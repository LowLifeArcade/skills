# 90s Japan

Treat the requested artifact as if it emerged from Japan between 1990 and 1999. Preserve what the artifact must do; transform how it feels.

## Direct the output

1. Identify the artifact's purpose, audience, medium, and non-negotiable constraints.
2. Choose one primary lane from `references/style-system.md`. Add at most one secondary lane.
3. Select a specific implied year or short period. Early-, mid-, and late-1990s should not look interchangeable.
4. Translate the era through medium-specific choices: composition, typography, color, texture, objects, language, pacing, sound, or interaction.
5. Include several concrete period signals, but leave enough restraint for the artifact to remain coherent.
6. Verify that the result still works: facts remain true, code functions, text communicates, and accessibility is not sacrificed for atmosphere.

## Set the intensity

Infer an intensity unless the user specifies one:

- **Trace**: Keep the base design; add a few era-aware details.
- **Balanced**: Make the influence immediately legible without overwhelming the content. Use this by default.
- **Full immersion**: Build the artifact around the era, including material texture, period conventions, and bolder composition.

## Adapt by medium

- **Web and UI**: Produce functional, responsive, accessible interfaces. Express the era through layout, type, controls, borders, image treatment, and microcopy; do not simulate poor usability merely because old software did.
- **Images and video**: Describe camera, stock, lighting, wardrobe, signage, props, location, framing, texture, and post-processing. Prefer observed details over a generic `retro anime` label.
- **Writing and concepts**: Convey the setting through concrete social, technological, commercial, and sensory details. Keep factual or professional prose accurate.
- **Branding and packaging**: Define a small system—mark behavior, type roles, palette, graphic devices, substrate, and production finish—rather than adding isolated motifs.
- **Slides and documents**: Preserve hierarchy and legibility. Use the aesthetic in title treatments, dividers, diagrams, photography, labels, and restrained texture.
- **Audio and music direction**: Specify instruments, arrangement, production texture, tempo, listening context, and emotional register without imitating a living artist or reproducing a copyrighted song.

## Guardrails

- Do not reduce Japan to neon, kanji, anime, sushi, or cyberpunk. Use a coherent slice of the decade.
- Do not combine every nostalgic signal. Prefer a few mutually reinforcing choices.
- Do not use meaningless Japanese text. Write correct Japanese when appropriate, verify uncertain language, or use English and numerals instead.
- Do not claim historical authenticity when inventing details. Distinguish period-inspired work from documentary reconstruction.
- Do not copy a named artist, franchise, logo, character, or existing composition. Translate high-level period characteristics into an original result.
- Do not alter the user's facts, required content, product constraints, or output format merely to serve the theme.
- Do not add visible explanation of the styling process unless the user asks for rationale. Deliver the themed artifact directly.

## Finish with a coherence pass

Check that the palette, typography, language, objects, texture, and tone plausibly belong to the same lane and moment. Remove any element that reads as contemporary nostalgia pasted onto an otherwise unrelated output.

Read `references/style-system.md` before producing the themed work. Use it to select a lane, signals, palette, and medium-specific vocabulary.

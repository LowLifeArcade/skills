# 1990s Japan style system

Use this reference as a selection system, not a checklist. Choose one primary lane and at most one secondary lane. Favor internal coherence over maximum nostalgia.

## Period calibration

### Early 1990s — 1990–1993

- Mood: confident, polished, metropolitan, with the last glow of bubble-era optimism
- Form: generous geometry, glossy surfaces, strong grids, elegant editorial spacing
- Technology: fax paper, landlines, cassette players, CRT displays, early laptops, large stereo components
- Color: black, cream, lacquer red, teal, cobalt, gold accents

### Mid-1990s — 1994–1996

- Mood: playful, compact, street-aware, increasingly digital
- Form: stickers, cropped type, saturated blocks, rounded plastic, denser layering
- Technology: pagers, mini components, game centers, 8–16-bit carryover, early consumer digital interfaces
- Color: electric blue, warm yellow, tomato red, mint, smoky gray

### Late 1990s — 1997–1999

- Mood: wired, translucent, kinetic, optimistic with a faint Y2K edge
- Form: chrome, bubbles, capsule shapes, liquid gradients, tiny technical labels
- Technology: flip phones, MiniDisc, compact digital cameras, dial-up web, translucent electronics, rhythm games
- Color: ice blue, violet, silver, acid lime, white, deep navy

## Creative lanes

### 1. Tokyo after dark

Use for nightlife, fashion, music, hospitality, cinematic scenes, and atmospheric interfaces.

- Signals: rain-dark asphalt, fluorescent convenience stores, last-train clocks, taxi upholstery, compact bars, vending-machine light, telephone booths
- Composition: cinematic crops, deep shadow, bright practical lights, quiet negative space
- Texture: 35mm grain, halation, slight underexposure, timestamp or small caption used sparingly
- Avoid: a wall of random neon kanji, futuristic megacity clichés, default cyan-magenta cyberpunk

### 2. Consumer-tech optimism

Use for products, SaaS, dashboards, electronics, technical branding, and speculative objects.

- Signals: molded plastic, tiny model numbers, status LEDs, instruction labels, segmented displays, removable media, coiled cables
- Composition: product-catalog precision, exploded diagrams, compact control clusters, generous industrial whitespace
- Texture: coated paper, screen-print ink, brushed metal, translucent shell, low-resolution LCD pixels
- Avoid: turning every control into an unusable skeuomorph or mixing devices from distant decades

### 3. Print, catalog, and magazine

Use for editorial work, decks, posters, packaging, menus, portfolios, and content-heavy pages.

- Signals: vertical side notes, issue numbers, price circles, registration marks, small captions, product cutouts, tightly art-directed photography
- Composition: asymmetric grid, dramatic scale contrast, image crops that touch an edge, deliberate information density
- Texture: uncoated stock, halftone, risograph-like misregistration, photocopy contrast, glossy insert
- Avoid: fake Japanese filler and indiscriminate collage

### 4. Arcade and youth energy

Use for games, events, playful brands, motion, sports, and music interfaces.

- Signals: token slots, score tables, rhythm cues, sticker graphics, mascot-like geometric forms, prize-counter colors, transit-card energy
- Composition: diagonal movement, modular panels, punchy badges, chunky counters, controlled visual bursts
- Texture: CRT scanlines, pixel edges, vinyl decals, plastic buttons, thermal-ticket print
- Avoid: copying recognizable game characters, cabinets, logos, or anime properties

### 5. Everyday Heisei nostalgia

Use for stories, lifestyle work, intimate brands, photo essays, food, travel, and quiet interfaces.

- Signals: apartment balconies, neighborhood shops, school stationery, family restaurants, commuter platforms, floral curtains, compact kitchens, printed snapshots
- Composition: human-scale framing, soft observation, imperfect symmetry, breathing room
- Texture: drugstore film, date imprint, faded paper, fluorescent indoor cast, handwritten annotation
- Avoid: presenting ordinary life as exotic scenery or making every scene sentimental

## Typography

- Prefer period-plausible grotesk sans, condensed display sans, Mincho-style serif, rounded gothic, monospaced technical faces, or bitmap accents according to the lane.
- Establish roles: one display face, one reading face, and optionally one numeric or pixel accent.
- Use Japanese typography only when the text is correct. Respect vertical writing, punctuation, spacing, and line-breaking conventions when employing them.
- Use small English technical labels, catalog numbers, dates, and prices as supporting texture—not as meaningless noise.
- Avoid modern default neo-grotesk minimalism unless counterbalanced by period-specific composition and material detail.

## Color recipes

Treat these as starting points and adjust for contrast:

- Metropolitan polish: `#111318`, `#F1EBDD`, `#B6292E`, `#156B75`, `#C8A45B`
- Game-center pop: `#142A63`, `#F4C928`, `#E94B3C`, `#56BFA8`, `#F7F2E8`
- Late-digital: `#151B38`, `#DDECF2`, `#8C6AC8`, `#B7D63D`, `#AEB7C2`
- Everyday film: `#5E6658`, `#D6C5A5`, `#A85B4B`, `#6F8C91`, `#EFE6D5`
- Night street: `#0A1117`, `#1C5560`, `#D84B78`, `#E7B84B`, `#DCE9E6`

For functional interfaces, test text and control contrast. Texture must never obscure essential information.

## Material and image vocabulary

Choose three to six relevant signals:

- analog grain, halation, fluorescent color cast, flash falloff
- halftone dots, ink spread, registration drift, photocopy crush
- smoked or translucent plastic, brushed aluminum, chrome trim
- CRT bloom, segmented LCD, bitmap cursor, scanline restraint
- thermal paper, ticket stock, sticker residue, clear vinyl sleeve
- date stamps, catalog codes, route diagrams, compact price labels

## Language and tone

- Keep prose concise, observant, and concrete.
- Let labels and microcopy feel useful first; period flavor comes from rhythm, categorization, and specificity.
- Favor exact objects and situations over vague adjectives such as `retro`, `vintage`, `vibes`, or `aesthetic`.
- When the subject is not Japanese, apply the creative direction without falsely relocating it or pretending it is Japanese-made.

## Fast selection examples

- A weather app: late-digital primary + consumer-tech optimism; segmented readings, tiny station codes, translucent panels, accessible modern navigation.
- A coffee package: print/catalog primary + everyday nostalgia; restrained product photography, price-sticker geometry, warm paper, correct small Japanese copy only if supplied or verified.
- A detective story cover: Tokyo after dark primary + print/editorial secondary; rain reflection, compressed serif title, small issue metadata, film grain.
- A quarterly business deck: early-1990s print/catalog; strong grid, cobalt and cream, catalog-like charts, minimal texture, contemporary data clarity.
- A music event page: arcade/youth primary + late-digital secondary; rhythm-grid motion, ticket numerals, silver and acid-lime accents, no borrowed characters.
